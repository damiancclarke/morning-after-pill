\section{Methodology}
We are interested in estimating the effect of being a teenage mother---that is to say 
giving live birth (\emph{LB}) to a child---on later life outcomes of the mother.  We
examine three types of outcome variables (below denoted as $y$).  These are: labour market
outcomes, educational outcomes, and future child-bearing patterns.  In order to identify
the effect of teenage child bearing, we estimate the following equation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:teenbirth}
y_{ict}=\beta_0+\beta_1LB_{ict}+\beta_2 Bwt_{ict} + \beta_3 gest_{ict} + \gamma\mathbf{X}_{ict} + \alpha_c + \eta_t + \varepsilon_{ict},
\end{equation}
where $\beta_1$ denotes our estimator of interest for mother $i$, living in \emph{comuna} $c$, who
gave birth in year $t$.  We define our `treatment' variable $LB$ as follows:
\[
 LB_{ict} =
  \begin{cases}
   1 & \text{if } TeenPreg=1, miscarriage=0   \\
   0 & \text{if } TeenPreg=1, miscarriage=1.
  \end{cases}
\]
Here we identify the effect of teen pregnancy by comparing two groups of women who become
pregnant as teens: those who eventually give birth to children (and hence are treated), and
those who do not give live birth due to miscarriage (and hence act as controls).  An important
concern here is that miscarriage is not random, but rather depends upon maternal health
characteristics and behaviours which will also be correlated with later outcomes (see for example
\citet{Hotzetal1997, FletcherWolfe2009} for discussions).

In order to control for these health behaviours of mothers, we include a measure of weight at
birth and gestational length in equation (\ref{eqn:teenbirth}).  In this case our identifying 
assumption is that:
\[
 miscarriage_{ict}\Big|(gest_{ict},Bwt_{ict})\independent \varepsilon_{ict},
\]
which is to say that maternal health characteristics and behaviours during pregnancy are adequately 
captured by the birthweight and gestational length of their baby.
